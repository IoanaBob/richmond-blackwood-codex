## Workflow

1. Gather the product, buyer, objective, claims, proof, legal wording, and open questions before writing slides.
2. If the user provides reference decks, inspect them visually. If a connector is blocked, record the blocker and continue only from the references that were actually inspected.
3. Draft each slide as:
   - Eyebrow: common deck/navigation label, max 3 words.
   - Headline: a complete, understandable selling claim.
   - Body: 0-2 short sentences, used only to set up the visual.
   - Visual structure: cards, proof numbers, icons, chips, timeline, process, mockup, table, or chart.
   - Slide-local appendix: sources, calculations, caveats, operating detail, and extra notes belonging to that slide.
4. Review every slide against the QA checklist before presenting a packet or creating the deck in Figma.
5. Do not move into Figma creation until the written packet passes the slide-by-slide checklist.

## House Style

- Use 16:9 slides unless the user specifies otherwise.
- Put an eyebrow or section label at top left and the slide number at top right.
- Eyebrows are for orientation, not selling. Use common deck labels by default, such as `Problem`, `Why Us`, `How It Works`, `Use Cases`, `Case Studies`, `Packages`, `Deliverable`, and `Next Steps`.
- Make the title do real work. Avoid empty labels such as "Trust, judgment, and authorization" when the title can state the commercial point.
- Use concise body copy. If a slide needs evidence, assumptions, or calculations, place them in that slide's appendix.
- Spread meaning across the slide. Use proof cards, labels, process steps, chips, callouts, mockups, and small captions instead of one large subtitle block.
- Keep visual text surfaces compact. If using columns, avoid text-heavy columns on both sides.
- Use plain language that a buyer would say. Prefer "follow-through", "next step", "reviewed answer", or "specialist review" over invented jargon.
- Use audience-understandable entities. For a US-facing proof point, say "US Tax Authorities" or "IRS" instead of assuming the buyer knows foreign agencies.
- Use credible proof. Cite public sources, local source packets, or clearly labelled assumptions. Do not make a model look like measured data.
- Keep appendices with the slide they support, not as a disconnected end section.

## Writing Rules

- Headlines should be specific and different from subtitles, body copy, and labels. Do not repeat the same key phrase across those fields.
- Treat the eyebrow and headline as separate jobs: eyebrow names the slide type, headline sells the point.
- Avoid contrast constructions such as "not X; it is Y" or "not X, but Y" in pitch copy. State the positive claim directly.
- Avoid customer-negative words as product promises. For example, do not sell a "blocker"; sell the reviewed outcome, next action, escalation path, or answer.
- Prefer "our team" when explaining managed service delivery, especially when repeating the product name would sound impersonal.
- Body copy should usually be one sentence. Two sentences is the limit unless the user explicitly asks for a narrative slide.
- Titles should not waste the strongest slot. If the title can answer "why should I care?", rewrite it.
- After every revision, re-check the whole deck against the user's latest comments instead of only fixing the one named slide.

## Reference Patterns From Exotic Vaults

Use these as adjacent examples, not rigid templates:

- Cover: dark, confident title slide with the company/product as the first-viewport signal.
- Agenda: compact table of contents, no long prose.
- Credibility slide: central mockup or image with floating metric cards around it.
- Team slide: person cards with names, roles, and short credibility proof.
- Problem slide: one claim headline plus three strong problem cards with short labels and a proof-oriented caption.
- Solution slide: a visual process through the middle with short step labels and benefit cards below.
- Market or opportunity slide: large numbers in cards with source notes in the slide-local appendix.
- Strategy slide: visual sequence, table, or map with short labels.
- Roadmap slide: timeline with milestones, dates, and concise proof of progress.
- Closing slide: direct ask, contact details, and minimal supporting copy.

## Slide-Type Guidance

- Problem slides: show the problem and where time, risk, or money is lost. Do not show the solution as the main graphic.
- Why-now or why-delegation slides: explain why the work was historically kept in-house, then show why RB changes the trust equation.
- Solution slides: show what the client gives RB, what the team verifies, how calls and escalation happen, how specialist review works, and what outcome the client receives.
- Process slides: merge overlapping steps. Calls and escalation can be one step when the buyer experiences them as follow-through.
- Case-study slides: use side-by-side cards with situation, specialist handling, action, and English outcome.
- Pricing or package slides: show a package ladder, credit meter, inclusions, and pilot terms. Keep price detail out if the user asks to avoid explicit pricing.
- Outcome/report slides: use a report mockup so transcript, summary, translation, and next action are visible without a paragraph.

## QA Checklist

Before sharing a draft, check every slide:

- Eyebrow is a common deck/navigation label and 3 words or fewer.
- Headline is a clear claim, not a topic label.
- Body is 0-2 sentences.
- The slide does not rely on a huge subtitle to carry the message.
- Text is distributed across visual elements.
- No key phrase is repeated between headline, subtitle, body, and labels.
- No contrast construction is used as pitch copy.
- Any proof is sourced or clearly labelled as an assumption.
- The visual direction matches the user's requested purpose for that slide.
- Appendix notes sit under the relevant slide.
- The slide would still make sense if presented quickly.

## Figma Handoff

When creating the deck in Figma or Figma Slides, use the Figma skills and connector workflow. Build from the approved written packet, preserve the slide structure, and use the same text hierarchy: eyebrow, claim headline, compact body, visual text surfaces, and slide-local appendix notes.
